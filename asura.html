For my senior design project, my group and I developed a UAV system for search and rescue teams. For our work, we received the Honorable Harold Berger Award from the Electrical and Systems Engineering department and placed sixth out of eighteen interdepartmental finalists (honorable mention). We designed our system to be easy to extend with additional sensors, different vehicles, and multiple agents. One of my primary contributions to the project was the person detection and tracking algorithm demonstrated in the figure, which I wrote in Python using OpenCV based on this paper.

At the heart of the detection / tracking algorithm is a particle filter — a Bayesian algorithm often used to identify the state of a system give some observed output, a heuristic for evaluating how good a particular state and a noisy process model. In this case, the particles are objects called silhouettes, or pairs of ellipses arranged to fit the outline of a human (shown in figure). In order to evaluate whether or not a silhouette is well fit to a human, the image gradient along the ellipses is summed. The particle filter maintains a bank of silhouettes, and with each new frame, the silhouettes are updated and scored. Low scoring silhouettes are regenerated to keep the "genetic diversity" of the system high while high scoring particles signify objects that are likely human.
